# General micro-tips shown across morning/afternoon/evening, one per line.
Eye doctors have recommended the 20-20-20 rule for years: every 20 minutes, look at something roughly 20 feet away for 20 seconds. No study has actually proven those exact numbers, just that regular breaks help.
A full, deliberate blink does more for dry eyes than a fast one does. One study found how completely you blink matters more than how often, and screens quietly cut blinks short.
Stand up, stretch your legs out, and let your shoulders drop for a second.
Uncross your legs and roll your ankles a few times right now.
Take three slow, deep breaths. Slowing your breathing like this can shift your nervous system out of alert mode surprisingly fast.
Try matching your screen brightness to the room around you. Constantly re-adapting between a bright screen and a dim room is its own source of eye strain.
A five-minute walk, even just laps around the room, is often enough to reset a tired focus.
Try to fit in a few screen-free breaks during your study blocks, three or so if you can manage it.
Keep a glass of water within reach on your desk so sipping doesn't take any real effort.
Roll your shoulders backward a handful of times to loosen whatever tension has built up.
Check in on your jaw and shoulders now and then. It's an easy spot for tension to build without you noticing until it's already there.
Take a moment to check your posture: ears, shoulders, and hips more or less stacked on top of each other.
Glare is easy to overlook, but it's one of the more common causes of eye strain, so it's worth adjusting your screen if you can.
If you've been typing a while, wiggle your fingers and stretch your wrists out.
A short stretch break tends to leave you noticeably more refreshed once you sit back down, more than the minute it costs you.
Pay attention to your breathing for a second. If it feels shallow, a few slower breaths usually help more than you'd expect.
Adjust your chair so your feet actually rest flat on the floor, rather than dangling or reaching for it.
A minute without any screen or sound can feel strangely calming if you give it an honest try, almost like your brain doesn't know what to do with the quiet.
If you've hit a natural pause in what you're doing, that's usually a good moment to take a short break.
Loosen your grip on the mouse or keyboard. People tend to grip far harder than the task actually requires.
Standing up every so often, even briefly, helps offset a long stretch of sitting.
Try positioning your screen at eye level and about an arm's length away. It eases both neck and eye strain at once.
Let your chair's backrest actually hold your lower back instead of leaning forward off of it all day.
Mild dehydration can dull your focus well before you ever feel thirsty, so a sip of water might help more than it seems like it should.
Gently stretch your neck from side to side for a moment to ease whatever tension has built up there.
Try to keep your shoulders down and relaxed instead of creeping up toward your ears, a classic tell for stress.
If a screen feels too close, bumping up the text size usually beats leaning in toward it.
Bent wrists measurably raise the pressure inside your carpal tunnel while you type. Keeping them straight is a small change with a real effect.
Changing position now and then, sitting, standing, or walking, tends to ease physical fatigue better than staying put ever does.
A cluttered desk isn't just distracting in a vague sense. Each object in view competes with the others for attention in your visual cortex.
Every extra hour of sitting is linked to worse long-term health markers, per a review of 47 studies, even among people who exercise regularly.
Switching between tasks has a measurable cost in psychology research. Finishing one thing before starting the next is usually faster overall.
Even a quick glance at a notification pulls your focus away, and refocusing afterward can take twenty minutes or more in some studies.
A view of greenery, even through a window, helps recover from mental fatigue. Psychologists call the effect "soft fascination."
A stuffy room can climb past 1,000 ppm CO2, the point where one study found decision-making measurably starts to suffer.
Stress often shows up first as physical tension in the jaw, neck, and shoulders, sometimes measurably so before anyone feels stressed at all.
Sustained poor posture is linked to more fatigue over the course of a day, on top of the discomfort itself.
Media multitasking is linked to more reported stress rather than less, even though it can feel productive in the moment.
A Stanford study found walking, even just indoors, roughly doubled creative output versus sitting, and some of the boost lingered after sitting back down.
Daytime light exposure helps anchor your body clock too, not only the light you get first thing in the morning.
