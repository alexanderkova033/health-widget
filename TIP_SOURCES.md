# Scientific sources for tip content

The tips shown in the widget (`core/src/main/resources/tips/*.txt`) are short, hedged
suggestions ("can help," "is linked to"), not medical claims. This document records the
research behind each one, grouped by theme, so the wording can be checked or updated as
evidence changes. It is documentation only — it isn't read by the app.

## Eyes and screens

**20-20-20 rule** (general.txt) — Endorsed by the American Academy of Ophthalmology and
American Optometric Association as a way to reduce digital eye strain. Worth noting: no
peer-reviewed trial has validated the specific 20/20/20 numbers, and one study found the
exact protocol ineffective at relieving symptoms — which is why the tip says "many eye
doctors recommend" rather than claiming it's proven. The broader principle (regular visual
breaks reduce strain) is well supported even where the specific numbers aren't.
- [AOA 20-20-20 rule](https://www.aoa.org/AOA/Images/Patients/Eye%20Conditions/20-20-20-rule.pdf)
- [Optometry Times: Deconstructing the 20-20-20 rule](https://www.optometrytimes.com/view/deconstructing-20-20-20-rule-digital-eye-strain)

**Incomplete blinks and dry eye** (general.txt) — Portello et al. 2013 found blink
completeness correlated with dry-eye symptom severity more than blink rate alone.
- Portello, Rosenfield & Chu, *Blink Rate, Incomplete Blinks and Computer Vision Syndrome*,
  Optometry and Vision Science, 2013. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23538437/)

**Blink rate drops with screen/cognitive load** (afternoon.txt) — Cognitive demand, not the
screen itself, suppresses blink rate; screen reading measurably lowers blink rate versus
resting baseline.
- [ScienceDirect: Cognitive demand, digital screens and blink rate](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0747563215003829)

**Matching screen brightness to the room** (general.txt) — Visual-ergonomics guidance:
when a screen is much brighter than its surroundings, eyes constantly re-adapt between the
two, contributing to dryness, blur, and headaches; the standard fix is to bring screen
brightness roughly in line with ambient room light rather than maximizing either in
isolation.
- [UBC Visual Ergonomics: Solutions for Lighting & Eye Health](https://hr.ubc.ca/wellbeing-benefits/files/Visual-ergonomics-resources.pdf)

**Ergonomic screen distance/eye level, chair, wrist posture** (general.txt) — Standard
occupational-health guidance, not a single study.
- [OSHA eTools: Computer Workstations](https://www.osha.gov/etools/computer-workstations/components/monitors)
- [Cornell University Ergonomics Web](https://ergo.human.cornell.edu/DEA6510/dea6512k/ergo12tips.html)

**Neutral wrist posture and carpal tunnel pressure** (general.txt) — Non-neutral wrist
postures (extension, ulnar deviation) measurably raise carpal tunnel pressure.
- Rempel et al., *Effect of Wrist Posture on Carpal Tunnel Pressure while Typing*.
  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18383144/)

## Breathing, stress, and tension

**Slow breathing and vagal tone/HRV** (general.txt, afternoon.txt) — Slow-paced breathing
(≤6 breaths/min) reliably increases heart-rate variability and parasympathetic
(vagal) activity, and is linked to reduced cortisol and anxiety.
- [Systematic review & meta-analysis, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0149763422002007)

**Cold water on the face / "dive reflex"** (morning.txt) — Facial cold-water immersion
triggers the trigeminal nerve, activating a vagally-mediated drop in heart rate (the
mammalian dive reflex) — a real, measurable autonomic response, used clinically (e.g. in
DBT's TIPP skill) to downshift acute arousal.
- [StatPearls: Physiology, Diving Reflex](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK538245/)
- [PMC: Resting heart rate affects response to cold-water face immersion](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10295257/)

**Cold showers and alertness** (morning.txt) — Cold exposure raises norepinephrine
(driving alertness) while cortisol tends to stay flat or drop; framed cautiously since
longer-term-benefit research is thinner than the acute-alertness evidence.
- [PMC: Cardiovascular and mood responses to cold water immersion](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0306456523002681)

**Stress and tension in the jaw/neck/shoulders** (general.txt) — EMG studies show sustained
low-level contraction in the trapezius and related neck/shoulder muscles under
psychological stress (independent of physical workload) — a real, measurable
stress→muscle-tension pathway, not just a figure of speech.
- Lundberg et al., *Psychophysiological stress responses, muscle tension, and neck and
  shoulder pain among supermarket cashiers*, 1999. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10431284/)

## Sitting, movement, and posture

**Sedentary time as an independent health risk** (general.txt) — Biswas et al.'s
meta-analysis (47 studies) found sedentary time associated with higher all-cause
mortality and cardiovascular/diabetes risk *independent of* physical activity level.
- Biswas et al., *Sedentary Time and Its Association With Risk for Disease Incidence,
  Mortality, and Hospitalization in Adults*, Annals of Internal Medicine, 2015.
  [Author summary, SBRN](https://www.sedentarybehaviour.org/2015/03/11/author-summary-of-sedentary-time-and-its-association-with-risk-for-disease-incidence-mortality-and-hospitalization-in-adults-paper-published-in-the-annals-of-internal-medicine-20/)

**Breaking up sitting with brief standing/movement** (general.txt, afternoon.txt) —
Consistent with the above: since the risk is tied to *sedentary time* rather than total
activity, interrupting sitting periodically is the specific behavior the evidence supports.

**Sustained poor posture and fatigue over the day** (general.txt) — Office-worker studies
find perceived discomfort in the neck, shoulders, and low back rising significantly within
just an hour of sitting, with poor posture specifically (not sitting alone) linked to
increased low-back-pain risk.
- [ScienceDirect: Perceived musculoskeletal discomfort during prolonged sitting](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0003687020301770)

## Attention, focus, and cognitive breaks

**Task-switching cost** (general.txt) — Switching between tasks reliably costs measurable
time, increasing with task complexity/unfamiliarity.
- Rubinstein, Meyer & Evans, *Executive Control of Cognitive Processes in Task Switching*,
  J. Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance, 2001.
  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11518143/)

**Notifications and attention residue** (general.txt) — Interrupted work is done faster but
with more stress, and refocusing after an interruption takes meaningfully longer than the
interruption itself; the tip avoids citing an exact minute figure since estimates vary
across studies (commonly cited figures range from several minutes up to ~23–25 minutes).
- Mark, Gudith & Klocke, *The Cost of Interrupted Work: More Speed and Stress*, CHI 2008.
  [ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1357054.1357072)

**Visual clutter competes for attention** (general.txt) — Multiple objects in the visual
field mutually suppress each other's neural representation in visual cortex — a real
capacity limit, not just a feeling of being overwhelmed.
- McMains & Kastner, *Interactions of Top-Down and Bottom-Up Mechanisms in Human Visual
  Cortex*, J. Neuroscience, 2011. [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21228167/)

**Brief mental breaks restore vigilance** (afternoon.txt) — Brief task-switches/diversions
prevent the "vigilance decrement" that builds during sustained monotonous attention.
- Ariga & Lleras, *Brief and Rare Mental "Breaks" Keep You Focused*, Cognition, 2011.
  [ScienceDaily summary](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2011/02/110208131529.htm)

**Nature views and mental fatigue recovery** (general.txt) — Attention Restoration Theory:
directed attention is a depletable resource, and "soft fascination" (e.g. greenery, a
window view) lets it recover; office workers with a nature view report fewer ailments and
more job satisfaction.
- [Systematic review, Attention Restoration Theory, ECEHH](https://www.ecehh.org/research/attention-restoration-theory-a-systematic-review/)

**Indoor CO₂ and cognition** (general.txt) — Controlled-chamber study found decision-making
performance measurably worse at 1,000 ppm CO₂ and much worse at 2,500 ppm, versus a 600 ppm
baseline — independent of ventilation as a confound.
- Satish et al., *Is CO2 an Indoor Pollutant?*, Environmental Health Perspectives, 2012.
  [Berkeley Lab summary](https://newscenter.lbl.gov/2012/10/17/elevated-indoor-carbon-dioxide-impairs-decision-making-performance/)

**Media multitasking and stress** (general.txt) — A large German probability sample found
communication load and internet multitasking positively associated with perceived stress
and indirectly with burnout/anxiety/depression.
- Reinecke et al., *Digital Stress over the Life Span*, Media Psychology, 2017.
  [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/296623540)

**Walking and creativity** (general.txt) — Walking (indoors or outdoors) roughly doubled
creative-response output versus sitting, in a controlled Stanford study; the effect
persisted briefly after sitting back down.
- Oppezzo & Schwartz, *Give Your Ideas Some Legs*, J. Experimental Psychology: Learning,
  Memory, and Cognition, 2014. [Stanford News](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2014/04/walking-vs-sitting-042414)

## Circadian rhythm and mornings

**Morning/outdoor light and circadian entrainment** (morning.txt) — Light is the primary
entrainment cue for the circadian clock; even brief morning bright-light exposure produces
a meaningful phase advance. Outdoor daylight is roughly an order of magnitude brighter than
typical indoor lighting, even on overcast days.
- [PMC: Circadian phase advance from morning light](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7029701/)
- [Illuminance comparison, EngineeringToolBox](https://www.engineeringtoolbox.com/light-level-rooms-d_708.html)

**Cortisol awakening response** (morning.txt) — Cortisol rises 60–150% in the ~30 minutes
after waking as a normal, functional part of preparing for the day — not itself a stress
signal, though it correlates with life/job stress levels.
- [ScienceDirect Topics: Cortisol Awakening Response overview](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/neuroscience/cortisol-awakening-response)

**Wake time as the stronger circadian anchor** (morning.txt) — Morning light exposure
(which follows a fixed wake time) is the primary daily calibration signal for the clock,
making consistent wake time a stronger anchor than bedtime.
- [Sleep Regularity Index cohort study, SLEEP/Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/sleep/article/47/1/zsad253/7280269)

**Breakfast-skipping and metabolism** (morning.txt) — Multiple controlled studies/meta-
analyses find no meaningful drop in 24-hour energy expenditure from skipping breakfast,
contradicting the popular "kick-starts metabolism" claim.
- [IFIC: 4 Metabolism Myths, Busted](https://ific.org/resources/articles/myths-about-your-metabolism/)

**Protein + carbs at breakfast and glucose stability** (morning.txt) — Protein-enriched
breakfasts blunt the post-meal glucose spike compared to carb-only breakfasts, with a
"second-meal effect" extending to lunch.
- [MDPI: High-protein breakfast and postprandial glucose](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/15/1/85)

**Morning exercise, alertness, and mood** (morning.txt) — Even brief, light-to-moderate
activity raises heart rate enough to increase arousal/alertness; acute exercise
meta-analyses show reliable mood improvement after a single bout.
- [Meta-analysis: Affective Responses to Acute Exercise](https://www.ovid.com/jnls/bsam/abstract/10.1097/psy.0000000000001321)

**Snoozing and sleep inertia** (morning.txt) — Using a snooze alarm prolongs sleep inertia
versus a single alarm; repeated forced awakenings interrupt REM/deep sleep stages tied to
mood and cognition.
- Sundelin et al., *Is snoozing losing?*, J. Sleep Research, 2024.
  [Wiley](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jsr.14054)

**Natural light and faster melatonin suppression** (morning.txt) — Higher-illuminance,
blue-white light (the profile of outdoor daylight, as opposed to dim/warm indoor lighting)
produces measurably greater melatonin suppression, part of why outdoor light helps you
feel awake sooner than an equivalent amount of time indoors.
- [PMC: Effects of day-time exposure to light intensity on melatonin suppression](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4491270/)

## Afternoons

**Post-lunch/early-afternoon dip is partly circadian** (afternoon.txt) — Monk's research
shows the dip occurs even without lunch and tracks a secondary (~12-hour harmonic) trough
in the circadian temperature/alertness rhythm, not just digestion.
- Monk, *The Post-Lunch Dip in Performance*, Chronobiology International.
  [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-post-lunch-dip-in-performance.-Monk/2a637623cc562fffcddf9aa8c41e41ef2069f3e1)

**Caffeine half-life and afternoon cups affecting bedtime** (afternoon.txt, evening.txt) —
Caffeine's average half-life is ~5 hours (range ~1.5–9.5h); 400mg taken 6 hours before
bed still significantly disrupted sleep in a controlled trial.
- Drake et al., *Caffeine Effects on Sleep Taken 0, 3, or 6 Hours before Going to Bed*,
  J. Clinical Sleep Medicine, 2013. [JCSM](https://jcsm.aasm.org/doi/10.5664/jcsm.3170)

**Nap length and grogginess / late naps and night sleep** (afternoon.txt) — Naps at
~20–26 minutes stay out of slow-wave sleep and avoid the worst sleep inertia (NASA's 1995
pilot-nap study found 26 minutes optimal); naps taken later in the day/after ~3pm are more
likely to reduce sleep drive and disrupt nighttime sleep onset.
- [Sleep Foundation: NASA Nap](https://www.sleepfoundation.org/sleep-hygiene/nasa-nap)
- [Sleep Foundation: Does Napping Impact Sleep at Night?](https://www.sleepfoundation.org/how-sleep-works/does-napping-impact-sleep-at-night)

**Bright light and afternoon alertness** (afternoon.txt) — Light exposure has a direct
alerting effect independent of vision, used experimentally to offset the post-lunch dip.
- [PubMed: Light intervention and the post-lunch dip](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30369519/)

**High-carb lunches and the afternoon crash** (afternoon.txt) — Refined-carbohydrate meals
can trigger an insulin overshoot and a subsequent drop in blood glucose (reactive
hypoglycemia) 1–5 hours after eating, producing fatigue — though this compounds with,
rather than replaces, the circadian post-lunch dip described above.
- [Wikipedia: Reactive hypoglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactive_hypoglycemia)

## Evenings and sleep

**Blue-enriched light and melatonin suppression** (evening.txt) — Reading on a
light-emitting device before bed (vs. a printed book) suppressed melatonin, delayed the
circadian clock, and reduced next-morning alertness in a controlled 2-week crossover study.
- Chang et al., *Evening use of light-emitting eReaders negatively affects sleep, circadian
  timing, and next-morning alertness*, PNAS, 2015.
  [Penn State summary](https://www.psu.edu/news/research/story/light-emitting-e-readers-detrimentally-shift-circadian-clock)

**Even dim light suppresses melatonin** (evening.txt) — Ordinary room light before bedtime
(not just bright/blue light) measurably suppressed melatonin onset and shortened its
duration.
- Gooley et al., *Exposure to Room Light before Bedtime Suppresses Melatonin Onset*, J.
  Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism, 2011.
  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21193540/)

**Warm bath/shower before bed** (evening.txt) — Meta-analysis of 13 studies: bathing in
40–42.5°C water 1–2 hours before bed (as little as 10 minutes) shortened sleep-onset
latency. Mechanism: warming the skin increases blood flow to hands/feet, which speeds heat
loss and helps core body temperature drop faster — the temperature drop is itself part of
the body's sleep-onset signal.
- Haghayegh et al., *Before-bedtime passive body heating by warm shower or bath to improve
  sleep*, Sleep Medicine Reviews, 2019.
  [ScienceDaily summary](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2019/07/190719173554.htm)

**Alcohol and sleep fragmentation** (evening.txt) — Alcohol shortens sleep-onset time via
sedation but reduces total REM sleep and increases wakefulness/fragmentation later in the
night — a consistent finding across meta-analyses even at moderate doses.
- [Meta-analysis: The effect of alcohol on subsequent sleep in healthy adults](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1087079224001345)

**Eating close to bedtime** (evening.txt) — Eating/drinking within an hour of bed was
linked to less efficient sleep; eating within 3 hours was linked to more frequent
awakenings.
- [AJMC: Eating or drinking up to 1 hour before bedtime may impair sleep quality](https://www.ajmc.com/view/eating-or-drinking-up-to-one-hour-before-bedtime-may-impair-sleep-quality)

**Short walk after a meal** (evening.txt) — A 10–15 minute walk taken near the post-meal
glucose peak (roughly 15–30 minutes after eating) measurably blunts the blood-glucose
spike; the "rest and digest" parasympathetic shift after eating is also when gut-brain
signaling most actively influences digestion and mood.
- [Nature Scientific Reports: 10-min walk after glucose intake](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-07312-y)

**Writing tomorrow's to-do list before bed** (evening.txt) — In a polysomnography (brain-
wave monitored) study, participants who spent 5 minutes writing a specific to-do list fell
asleep ~9 minutes faster on average than those who wrote about tasks they'd already
completed — more detailed lists correlated with falling asleep faster still.
- Scullin et al., *The Effects of Bedtime Writing on Sleep*, J. Experimental Psychology:
  General, 2018. [Baylor University](https://news.web.baylor.edu/news/story/2018/can-writing-your-dos-help-you-doze-baylor-study-suggests-jotting-down-tasks-can)

**Gratitude and sleep quality** (evening.txt) — Higher trait gratitude was associated with
better sleep quality, longer duration, and faster sleep onset, independent of personality
traits — mediated by fewer negative pre-sleep thoughts.
- Wood, Joseph, Lloyd & Atkins, *Gratitude influences sleep through the mechanism of
  pre-sleep cognitions*, J. Psychosomatic Research, 2009.
  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19073292/)

**Keeping the bed for sleep only** (evening.txt) — Stimulus control therapy (a core,
evidence-backed component of CBT-I) trains the bed as a specific cue for sleep by
eliminating incompatible activities in it.
- Bootzin (1972); see [Stanford Health Care: Stimulus Control and CBT-I](https://stanfordhealthcare.org/medical-treatments/c/cognitive-behavioral-therapy-insomnia/procedures/stimulus-control.html)

**Sleep regularity and health outcomes** (evening.txt) — A large UK cohort found consistent
sleep/wake timing associated with ~30% lower all-cause mortality risk for the most regular
sleepers vs. the least regular — a stronger association than sleep duration alone.
- *Sleep regularity is a stronger predictor of mortality risk than sleep duration*, SLEEP,
  2024. [PMC](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10782501/)

**Cool bedroom temperature** (evening.txt) — 65–68°F is the range commonly recommended by
sleep-health organizations, consistent with the natural pre-sleep drop in core body
temperature.
- [Sleep Foundation: The Best Temperature for Sleep](https://www.sleepfoundation.org/bedroom-environment/best-temperature-for-sleep)

## Hydration

**Mild dehydration and cognition/mood** (general.txt) — At ~1–2% body-mass fluid loss
(mild dehydration, well short of thirst-driven urgency), both mood and concentration
measurably worsen in controlled studies — the effect is present in both sexes, though the
specific symptoms differ (women showed larger mood effects; men showed more memory
degradation).
- Ganio et al., *Mild dehydration impairs cognitive performance and mood of men*, British
  Journal of Nutrition, 2011. [Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/mild-dehydration-impairs-cognitive-performance-and-mood-of-men/3388AB36B8DF73E844C9AD19271A75BF)
